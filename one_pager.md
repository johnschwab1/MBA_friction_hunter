# AI That Actually Teaches
### A structured approach to finding where AI creates real learning value in MBA curricula

---

## The problem with how AI is entering the classroom

Most AI adoption in business education right now is ad hoc: a professor tells students they can use ChatGPT, students use it to draft their work faster, and the learning outcome is unclear or actively worse. The tool is inserted without a theory of where it helps.

The research literature is more precise than the hype. AI creates genuine pedagogical value in specific, identifiable conditions — and undermines learning in others. The opportunity is to find the right spots before building anything.

---

## What this process does

We run a professor’s curriculum through a six-stage analysis pipeline, grounded in learning science, that outputs a small set of working prototypes worth testing.

**Stage 1 — Criteria**
We start with a rubric built from cognitive load theory, deliberate practice research, and the worked-example effect. It scores curriculum modules on five dimensions: teaching format, feedback requirements, contextual salience, learning mode, and friction tractability.

**Stage 2 — Friction Mapping**
We ingest the syllabus or course materials and classify where friction actually lives. Not all friction is bad — some is the learning. We identify five specific types that AI can address: feedback latency, repetition deficit, context poverty, reasoning opacity, and transfer failure.

**Stage 3 — Use Case Selection**
From the friction map, we select 2–4 use cases worth building. Selection criteria: learning impact, prototype feasibility, and whether the idea is interesting enough to show a faculty member in 15 minutes.

**Stage 4 — Architecture Search**
For each use case, we actively search current research — Stanford SCALE, MIT RAISE, Khan Academy’s prompt engineering team, recent arxiv publications — to find the right AI architecture. Not “give the student feedback,” but specifically: Socratic scaffolding with fading, analogical transfer mapping, retrieval practice loops, steelman stress-testing.

**Stage 5 — Design Brief**
For each use case we produce a one-page brief: what the friction is, what the AI does, a realistic dialogue example, and explicit success and failure signals. This is what we bring to the professor meeting.

**Stage 6 — Working Prototype**
Before showing a brief to a professor, we run a feasibility gate. If it passes, we generate a working system prompt ready to deploy in a Claude Project. The professor can interact with a real prototype in the same conversation where we’re discussing whether to build it.

## What the professor sees

A one-page design brief for each use case that shows:
- The specific friction in their course (stated in terms they’ll recognize)
- A realistic dialogue between a student and the AI
- What success looks like in a 15-minute test
- What failure looks like, so they know what to watch for
- Two or three questions we need them to answer to sharpen the prototype

If they want to see it live, we run the prototype on the spot.

---

## Where this is different

**From generic AI adoption:** We don’t start with the tool. We start with the friction.

**From research-only approaches:** Every output is a working prototype or a design brief backed by one, not a slide deck of possibilities.

**From basic prompt templates:** The architectures we source are grounded in published learning science — ZPD-based adaptive scaffolding, worked-example fading, analogical transfer — not prompts professors wrote in five minutes.

**Our specific lane:** Deliberate practice at behavioral scale and transfer failure via analogical variation. These are the two highest-value, least-solved problems in MBA education. Most AI tools don’t touch them. We are building directly into that gap.

---

## The research foundation

This process draws on:
- **Khanmigo / Khan Academy** — the most validated Socratic AI tutoring system, now at 700,000 users
- **SocraticAI** (MIT, 2024) — structured input protocols; students progress from vague to sophisticated in 2–3 weeks
- **Stanford SCALE / EDF Framework** — adaptive scaffolding with measurable fading and no overreliance
- **Cognitive Load Theory** (Sweller), **Desirable Difficulties** (Bjork), **Deliberate Practice** (Ericsson)
- **MetaLadder** (2025) — analogical transfer via structural problem mapping

## What we need from a professor to run the analysis

A syllabus, a slide deck outline, or a description of what they teach and how. That’s it. The analysis takes an afternoon. The design briefs are ready for the next conversation.
